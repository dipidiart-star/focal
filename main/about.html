About

Focal compression occurs as the distance between the lens and subject, causing optical distortions that appear as a sort of “flattening” effect. When the subject is still and the camera is framed such that the subject fills the same amount of space in the photo, the resulting image will have a seemingly flattened look as you progressively increase the focal length. This is why many photos taken by wide-angle lenses look distorted because the subject is so close to the lens that a “bulging” or “stretched” effect occurs. This is known as extension distortion. The opposite occurs when moving into telephoto lenses but not for the reason many think.

Misconceptions

Thus, the effect of focal compression isn’t necessarily products of lenses itself but instead the spatial relation from lens, subject and background. As the lens backs away from the subject, the field of view gets smaller however, since we are framing the shot to keep the subject the same size, it appears as though the background is getting enlarged. This then means a 100mm compared to a 16mm that is cropped in such that the subject fills the same amount of space will have the same background perception.

How this is used to our advantage

We can use these optical phenomena to incur a certain feeling in a shot. For example, to make a character appear crazy or insane, a wide-angle lens (avoiding focal compression) helps to invoke that “bizarre” feeling because things look distorted and unreal. However, a wide-angle lens can also be used to create contextual shots because of its wide field of view allowing for more info to be displayed on screen. On the opposite side of the spectrum, telephoto lenses can be used to create intimate scenes. Things appear “close” or “tight” which helps to invoke feelings of closeness or secrecy. The tight field of view information helps to separate the subject or subjects from the rest of the scene, almost creating a stalker-ish point of view.
